I've been self-studying Japanese alongside my CS work, targeting JLPT N5. This is a log of my 30-day challenge.

---

## Why Japanese?

A few reasons:

1. I'm building [KataYomi](https://github.com/thisizaro/KataYomi) — a Chrome extension for Katakana reading. Actually understanding Japanese makes me a better tool builder for it.
2. I like the challenge of a completely different script system.
3. It's fun.

---

## The Plan

- **Week 1:** Hiragana — all 46 characters
- **Week 2:** Katakana — all 46 characters
- **Week 3:** Basic vocabulary + numbers
- **Week 4:** Grammar particles + simple sentences

---

## Week 1: Hiragana

Started with mnemonics. Resources:

- **Tofugu's Hiragana guide** — best visual mnemonics I found
- **Anki** — spaced repetition for retention

Result: Hiragana memorized by Day 7. Reading slowly but accurately.

---

## Week 2: Katakana

Harder than Hiragana — the shapes feel more similar to each other. Common confusion pairs:

- ソ (so) vs ン (n)
- シ (shi) vs ツ (tsu)
- ワ (wa) vs ウ (u)

Trick that helped: **write them, don't just read them.** Physical writing made the shapes stick.

---

## Week 3: Vocabulary

Switched to **WaniKani free tier** + YouTube (JapanesePod101). Started with the 100 most common words.

Vocabulary is slower than scripts — requires more context to remember.

---

## Week 4: Grammar

**Genki I textbook** is the standard recommendation — used it for particle basics:

- は (wa) — topic marker
- が (ga) — subject marker
- を (wo) — object marker
- に (ni) — direction/time marker
- で (de) — location of action

---

## End of 30 Days

**Where I landed:**

- ✅ Hiragana — can read fluently
- ✅ Katakana — can read with some pausing
- 🔄 ~200 vocabulary words
- 🔄 Basic sentence structure (subject + object + verb)

**Current level:** JLPT N5 prep. Nowhere near test-ready but making consistent progress.

---

## Lessons

1. **Consistency beats intensity.** 20 minutes daily > 3 hours on weekends.
2. **Anki is essential** for vocabulary retention.
3. **Build things with the language** — making KataYomi gave me a real reason to care about Katakana.

Continuing. Long road.
